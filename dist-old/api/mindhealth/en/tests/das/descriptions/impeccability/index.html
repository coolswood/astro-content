The “Perfectionism” scale measures a person’s drive to be flawless in every area. A negative score suggests that the person demands absolute perfection from themselves: mistakes are unacceptable, any failure feels catastrophic, and even difficult emotions can be experienced as the end of the world.

They believe they must think and act perfectly; otherwise, they feel like a total failure. The race toward the ideal becomes an endless process: once one goal is reached, another immediately appears, and the anticipated joy of success never really comes. Life gradually loses its sense of pleasure and enjoyment because of unrealistic personal standards.

A positive score reflects the ability to set realistic goals and enjoy working toward them without fixating solely on the outcome. Such a person understands that they do not have to be perfect in everything and do not need to fear mistakes, seeing them instead as opportunities to learn and grow.

Paradoxically, these people are often more productive than their “perfect” counterparts, because they do not get stuck on every tiny detail. Their life is more like a flowing river, while the life of their perfectionistic friends can resemble “eternal ice”.
